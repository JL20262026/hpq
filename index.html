Introduction

In 1905, a revolution that changed Russia from an absolute monarchy to a semi-constitutional one, mainly due to a war with Japan the same year. The Russo-Japanese war was a largely naval conflict based around the sphere of influence in Manchuria’s southern regions and the city of Mukden, as well as naval conflicts around Tsushima. As a result of the war, a lack of faith in the autocracy, economic turmoil, as well as military exposure regarding outdated tactics, logistics and equipment, the ensuing loss of imperialistic opportunities in Asianic countries and the loss of influence will all be brought up and argued as to how and why they were significant into leading up to the 1905 revolution. In this essay I will discuss the factors from the Russo-Japanese war that led to the 1905 revolution in the Russian Empire, and evaluate which one was the most significant. Key Factors

1. Economic Disruption

One factor leading to the 1905 revolution via the Russo-Japanese war was the economic disruption caused by the conflict, which led to higher taxes, burdening the already distressed Russian peasants. The Russo-Japanese war, from February 1904, needed the ‘use of all financial means by the tsarist government. The war cost Russia, in total, 6.554 billion Rubles. Over half of this total, or 3.944 billion, paid the interest on foreign and domestic loans. This immense financial burden placed on the already strained Empire, which was also considered behind their European peers, meant it had to cancel state infrastructure projects and increase taxes, brewing resentment. Additionally, another reason why Russia’s economy suffered so greatly was their prior ‘peaceful’ economic penetration into Manchuria, Korea, and Mongolia. 2/3 of these locations being Korea and Manchuria were lost because of this war. This impacted Russia even more as its economy had grown massively due to these prospects growing from 415 million Rubles in 1867 to 1 billion Rubles in 1897. The loss of the war essentially ended Russia’s monopoly on East Asian exports in the region as well as ending their sphere of influence in Manchuria and Korea. This greatly reduced Russia’s revenue and would shut them out of the area, essentially removing them from an area exporting vital goods essential for Russia’s economy. Additionally, due to the Russo-Japanese war many commercial ports, supply lines and trade routes were occupied and lost, meaning the tsarist government further lost assets in the region. An example of this was on May 15th where the commercial port of Dalny was occupied by Japanese forces, which caused the Tsarist government to lose control of vital infrastructure and trade routes essential for commerce. This led overall to a complete loss of resources and jobs in Manchuria, which led to a downturn in the Russian Empire comparable to the one in 1900. All this economy strain put on by the war resulted in greatly increased taxation on the peasant serfdom, creating great resentment for the tsarist government, and making many disloyal and desiring for change, leading to the 1905 revolution which occurred but a year later. Overall, this is a significant factor that is a strong contender for the main cause of the 1905 revolution when regarding the Russo-Japanese war due to the large economic strain placed upon the subjects of the empire leading to people advocating for change and less loyalty towards the autocracy, culminating in the 1905 revolution.

2. Loss of Military Prestige

Another factor that led to the 1905 revolution was the loss of military prestige Russia faced and its humiliation as it was considered one of the major world powers at the time, encompassing a huge proportion of the globe. Its flawed leadership and outdated tactics and equipment, observed by the Russian public through their defeat, fuelled the fires for rebellion as the Russian people no longer viewed the Russian military as undefeatable, or in some cases, as a capable military force. The United States Corps of Engineers stated that ‘The conduct of the war on the part of the Russians was dependent-upon the operating capacity of the Siberia-Manchuria railroad, which has unjustly been much maligned.’ This statement reveals the dependent and incapable Russian logistics, and, when observed, can show their possible counterpart logistics capabilities if a revolution would arise. This revelation could have embolden many partisans into a more direct confrontation with the tsarist government. The Russian Empires outdated offensive tactics, particularly important in an uprising, were revealed to be inadequate as the same source stated that ‘General Mischtchenko 's raid to the rear of the Japanese army in the beginning of the second year of the war could not at all have caused its commanders any inconvenience’. This statement also reveals the militaries inadequate cavalry forces, which was a major revelation as during the time, as also revealed during WW1, cavalry was a major reason for success due to their ability to pull supply carts and transport infantry, as well as enabling skirmishing tactics and manoeuvrability of soldiers. This show of inadequate cavalry and harassing forces reveals a lack of speed and response time to the partisans, increasing their confidence in a potential conflict. Additionally, the Russian Empire’s flawed leadership which relied on outdated heroics of bravery, like in Mischtchenko’s cavalry charge, was exposed to the world through observations such as by the US Corps of Engineers, as shown in this source. This information led to the embodiment of reformists and other opposing parties to the tsarist government, yet again essentially adding fuel to the fire that was the growing discontent of the Russian peoples with the government. In conclusion, this exposure of Russia’s flawed leadership and outdated military tactics, as well as their loss of military prestige, emboldened partisans and opposing party members, and infuriated the people massively. These consequences make this a crucial factor of the 1905 revolution.

3. Loss of Eastern Territories

The third factor which led to the 1905 revolution via the Russo-Japanese war was the loss of eastern territories and influence in East Asia, which essentially ended Russia’s expansionist policy in the region and possibly the continent bar Siberia. In the peace treaty, named the treaty of Portsmouth due to the US city in which it was held in, resulted in Russia’s loss of the Liaodong peninsula (which held the vital naval base of port Arthur) as well as half of the Sakhalin island, not only expanding Japan’s territory but also at anger of the Russian peoples as this was considered a core territory of Russia, unlike their colonial post on the Liaodong peninsula. Russia also had to evacuate southern Manchuria, which led to a loss of territorial control and influence in the area. Furthermore, Japan gained control of another important imperial asset – the South Manchurian railway. This vital infrastructure leads to port Arthur and allows for important strategic redeployment in the region as well as transportation for materials and trade. These loss of territories enraged the Russian population and added to the growing discontent towards the tsarist government. This same treaty of Portsmouth ended Russia’s expansionist East Asia policy to establish hegemony over the whole of Asia. This outraged the Russian peoples as other European powers and indeed the public no longer viewed Russia as a rising imperial power due to its defeat by a previously signalisations country, solidified by their loss of territory and influence in East Asia. In conclusion, the loss of territories like the naval base of port Arthur and South Sakhalin Island as well as the loss of influence in the region when Russia formally recognised Japan’s dominion of Korea due to the treaty. This clear downgrade of Russia from a rising imperial power outraged the Russian peoples and showed a need for change, and the growing want and need for a revolution either successful or unsuccessful to facilitate change in the tsarist government.

4. Collapse of Faith in the Tsar

Finally, a fourth factor which led to the 1905 revolution because of the Russo-Japanese war was the loss of faith in Tsar Nicholas the second and the autocracy, potentially turning more to the idea of a new alternate government as the autocrats were not seen as infallible. Due to the outcome of the war, riots started which lasted for days, leading to the authorities instating martial law. In Russia, the defeat also justified the political oppositions criticism of the autocrat’s incompetent nature and the ideas of racial superiority and overconfidence which led to a decreased sense of caution. The unrest created by the war and the tsarist government’s inability to control it or stamp It out also led to growing support for other political parties such as liberals and a rise in nationalism in Russia’s ethnically diverse empire containing groups such as Ukrainians and Baltic populations such as Lithuanians, Estonians, and Latvians. Additionally, the loss of power the tsar had faced due to the devastating battle in which the Japanese had prevailed was shown once again at the treaty of Portsmouth which made the Tsar accept President Roosevelts mediation, greatly reducing his standing as leader of a supposed absolute monarchy of one of the biggest countries in the world at the time. Additionally, the lack of faith in the autocratic government was clearly shown as the minister of the interior was assassinated in 1904 while the war was undergoing. Plehve was killed in a terrorist bomb attack – he was also one of the main causes of the war, wanting a successful quick war to boost patriotism and moral in the empire. The fact such a high-ranking figure in the tsarist government was killed shows a complete erosion of faith and command in the country, and rising tensions. Overall, the loss of faith in the autocratic system and by extent the tsarist government led to a want for change and upheaval and reduced national moral as well as removing the illusion of the Tsardom as a father figure guiding the nation.

Comparative Analysis

Several factors contributed to the 1905 revolution because of the Russo-Japanese war. The first factor of economic disruption clearly burdened already strained peasants and caused public moral to drop significantly and allowed the people to see the cracks in the tsarist government, causing an increase in support for alternative governments such as socialism. The astonishing cost of 6.554 billion rubbles also reaffirmed the public’s perception about the tsarist government’s incompetency. The second factor regarding the loss of prestige and exposed military tactics bolstered the confidence in opposing governmental ideas of a revolution or revolt to weaken the tsarist government or overthrow it. The idea of the large tsarist military being invincible and able to protect the state from threats was also shattered, an attempt at revolution transition from an unlikely idea to one that seemed viable. The third factor on the loss of eastern territories and influence within Manchuria and Korea also contributed significantly to rising unrest which eventually led to the 1905 revolution. This is due to the public being able to visibly observe the territories lost and causes them to perceive the tsarist government as one incompetent and overly confident, as well as one that cannot protect its people from external threats due to the loss of core territories such as port Arthur and South Sakhalin. Lastly, the factor which showed a loss of faith in the tsar as a factor also shattered illusions regarding the aristocracy and contributed to rising rebellious tendencies. The aristocracy was no longer viewed as a competent and suitable government for the people by many, and the unifying dynasty of the Russian Tsars was substantially weakened, and this also caused rising nationalism in the diverse Russian Empire, (see figure 2 to see ethnic groups in 1914) which also caused even more of the public to turn against the tsarist government in support of their own nation, giving more support to opposing governments and militias, further leading to the 1905 revolution.

Conclusion

In conclusion, I believe all the factors I have analysed today have contributed significantly to the rise and subsequent beginning of the 1905 revolution. However, I also maintain the opinion that the most important factor regarding the 1905 revolutions start was the loss of military prestige as well as the exposure of outdated and rudimentary equipment the military was using in such a modern world. I believe this to be the case as all other factors caused a deal of unrest and desire for governmental change but would be obsolete without the exposure of military incompetence shown in the war – at this point the Russian Empire had status of a great power and a (on power at least) sizable and powerful military. Partisans would believe they lacked the power in both gear and manpower to take on such a foe, even with the support of the people. However, with the knowledge of the country’s military lacklustre, it was seen as an achievable goal, paving the way for the 1905 revolution.
